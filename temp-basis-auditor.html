Drift found

Across 15 California Current sites, the gridded index and in-situ temperature carry a site-specific offset of −0.14 to +0.45 °C whose sign is mixed — the index overpays at some sites and underpays at others, so no single correction fixes it. And the offset is not static: it drifts about −0.05 °C per year (pooled +0.25 °C in 2020 → −0.01 °C in 2025, and uncorrelated with warm/cool years), so a basis loading set once is measurably wrong within a few renewals.

Parametric temperature cover pays when a measured index crosses a rule — degrees above a threshold, marine-heatwave days. Because the index is a 25 km satellite cell and the loss happens in the water at the cages, the two can disagree; that disagreement is basis risk, and it is what makes a parametric policy pay when there was no loss, or stay silent when there was. This tool measures it — in the only unit that matters, payout-days — from real gridded and in-situ records, and shows how it moves over time. Every number recomputes as you move the controls.

Honest limits (post red-team)

  • Not fully independent instruments. OISST is bias-anchored to in-situ observations, so index and truth share a backbone. The point stands: a policy settles on one, the site lives the other.
  • The gap is threshold-relative. At any trigger it is roughly bias × (days the site spends near that trigger) — which is why we show the whole curve and never a single headline percentage.
  • Buoys are offshore open water, so this is a conservative lower bound; a nearshore cage site would diverge more, not less.
  • Materiality is tail-driven. The median site’s error is ~2 payout-days per season; the tail reaches ~10. The value is knowing which of your sites is in the tail.
  • The offset drifts, so it must be re-estimated each renewal — a static basis loading is the thing this audit is built to catch.
The engagement

What an underwriter would get from us

  1. A per-site basis & drift table for your book — signed offset, payout-day error at your trigger, and the drift rate, on your settlement index and your sites.
  2. A drift-aware basis loading — not a fixed number, but a band that says how fast it decays and when it needs re-estimating.
  3. Site triage — which locations sit in the material tail (and should carry a higher loading or a corrected index) and which are safe.
  4. Index bake-off — the same audit run on OISST vs MUR vs a reanalysis, so you settle on the index that minimises basis against the water your clients actually farm.
